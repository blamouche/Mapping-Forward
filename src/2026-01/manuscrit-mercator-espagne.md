# Un manuscrit decouvert en Espagne eclaire les origines de la projection de Mercator

## Elevator pitch

A professor from the University of Hasselt has discovered a lost Mercator manuscript hidden for over 450 years in Spain's El Escorial library, revealing the scientific foundations behind the famous cartographer's celestial globe and revolutionary mapping methods.

## Synthesis

A remarkable discovery has shed new light on the scientific methods of Gerardus Mercator, the Flemish cartographer whose map projection revolutionized navigation and remains in use today. Professor Koenraad Van Cleempoel from Belgium's University of Hasselt stumbled upon a manuscript thought lost to history while conducting research at the Royal Site of El Escorial, the historic Spanish royal residence near Madrid.

The discovery occurred when Van Cleempoel was handed a folder containing various manuscripts. Among them, he immediately recognized Mercator's distinctive handwriting. A seal on the document allowed researchers to date the manuscript to approximately 1550, placing it within the most productive period of Mercator's career. The document had remained hidden in plain sight for over 450 years within the El Escorial library, constructed between 1562 and 1584 at the behest of King Philip II of Spain.

The manuscript comprises four volumes containing roughly fifty pages of material that provides unprecedented insight into Mercator's scientific process. The contents include precise astronomical observations, innovative mathematical methods that Mercator developed, detailed plans for astronomical instruments, and three remarkable star tables. One of these stellar tables contains the precise coordinates of 130 stars and represents the scientific foundation upon which Mercator constructed his famous celestial globe of 1551.

Van Cleempoel describes the discovery as being of exceptional scope and importance. What makes the manuscript particularly valuable is its ability to reveal Mercator's thought process. "This document allows us to follow Mercator in his reasoning, his calculations, and his design. That is what makes it invaluable," the professor explains. Unlike published works that present polished final results, this manuscript shows the working methods and intellectual journey of one of history's most influential cartographers.

The find also carries broader significance for understanding the history of science. Van Cleempoel emphasizes that the discovery "reaffirms the historic role of the Low Countries in the scientific development of the Renaissance." The manuscript demonstrates the sophisticated astronomical and mathematical knowledge that underpinned the cartographic innovations for which Mercator is celebrated, including his famous 1569 world map projection that enabled navigators to plot courses using straight lines for the first time.

This represents the most complete document ever discovered in Mercator's own hand, offering scholars an unprecedented window into the working methods of Renaissance cartography's most transformative figure.
